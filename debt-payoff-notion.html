The average American has $104,000 in debt. The problem isn't usually income — it's visibility. When you can't see the total, the interest, and the timeline, debt feels permanent. Tracking it in Notion makes it a solvable problem with a concrete end date.

Snowball vs Avalanche

Two strategies, both work. The question is psychology vs math:

  • Snowball (Dave Ramsey) — pay off smallest balance first. Quick wins build momentum. You feel progress early. Mathematically suboptimal but psychologically powerful.
  • Avalanche — pay off highest interest rate first. Saves more money in total interest. Slower emotional payoff but faster financial payoff. Best if you're disciplined.

Notion can show both strategies side by side: total interest paid, debt-free date, and monthly payment schedule for each. Pick the one that matches your personality.

The Setup

  1. List every debt: credit cards, student loans, car loans, personal loans, medical debt
  2. For each: current balance, interest rate, minimum payment
  3. Calculate total debt (usually a shock — that's the point)
  4. Decide extra monthly amount you can throw at debt (even $50 helps)
  5. Notion calculates payoff order and dates for both strategies

The Extra Payment Effect

$30,000 in credit card debt at 22% APR with $600/month minimum: payoff in 8 years, $27,000 in interest. Add $200/month extra: payoff in 3.5 years, $12,000 in interest. That extra $200 saves $15,000 and 4.5 years. Seeing these numbers motivates consistent payments.

Monthly Check-In

Log every payment. Watch the balance drop. Celebrate milestones: first card paid off, below $20K, halfway point. The payment log turns abstract debt into a visible, shrinking number. That visibility is what keeps you going when month 14 feels exactly like month 1.
